How nasal strips work (and when they don't)

Short, self-contained answers to the questions people actually ask AI assistants.

What are nasal strips?

Nasal strips are spring-loaded adhesive bands worn across the bridge of the nose. As the band tries to straighten, it gently lifts the sides of the nose outward, widening the nasal valve, the narrowest part of the airway, so air moves more easily. They are drug-free, sold over the counter, and typically single-use.

Do nasal strips actually work for snoring?

They work when snoring starts in the nose, such as from congestion, allergies, or a narrow nasal valve, by widening the passage and reducing the airflow turbulence that vibrates tissue. They do not help snoring that comes from the soft palate, tongue, or obstructive sleep apnea. In our own testing, every nasal product improved airflow but none fully stopped our snoring, a sign the cause isn't purely nasal.

Nasal strips vs. nasal dilators: which is better?

Internal dilators expand the nostril from the inside and tend to deliver stronger, steadier airflow, a 2019 study found internal dilators outperformed adhesive strips. Adhesive strips are more comfortable, more hygienic, and easier to travel with. Reusable external dilators like Intake and Breathewave aim to split the difference. Pick by priority below.

Choose a strip if you want

  • The lowest upfront cost
  • Nothing inside your nose
  • Fresh, hygienic, single-use
  • Easy travel and no cleaning

Choose a dilator if you want

  • The strongest possible airflow
  • It to stay put when you move or sweat
  • Lower cost per night over time
  • No adhesive on your skin

Are nasal strips safe to use every night?

For most people, yes. The main downside of nightly adhesive use is skin irritation or, over months, mild redness where the adhesive pulls. Sensitive skin does better with gentle-adhesive strips like Dream Recovery or with a reusable dilator that uses no adhesive at all. Stop if you develop a rash.

Frequently asked questions

 

What is the best nasal strip for snoring?
In our hands-on testing so far, Intake and Breathewave gave the strongest nasal opening, Dream Recovery was the easiest to use, and Breathe Right is still the cheapest classic strip. Honestly, none fully solved our snoring, so the best pick depends on whether your snoring is truly nose-driven.
Do nasal strips help with sleep apnea?
No. Nasal strips and dilators improve nasal airflow but do not keep the airway open at the throat, which is where obstructive sleep apnea occurs. They may make CPAP more comfortable, but they are not a treatment. If you have or suspect apnea, talk to a doctor.
Why didn't any of them stop the snoring?
Because all of them work on the nose, and our snoring clearly doesn't start there. Nasal aids only help snoring caused by a congested or narrow nose. When the source is the soft palate, tongue, or jaw position, opening the nose more isn't enough, which is the whole reason we keep testing.
Is Intake worth it over Breathe Right?
If you snore most nights and your issue is nasal, the stronger opening and reusable band can justify it. Breathe Right wins on price and convenience for occasional use. Full breakdown in our Intake vs Breathe Right comparison.
Do nasal strips help with congestion and allergies?
Yes, mechanically. By widening the nasal valve they make a congested nose easier to breathe through, which is why they're marketed for cold and allergy relief. They don't reduce the underlying inflammation, pair them with an antihistamine or saline if allergies are the cause.
