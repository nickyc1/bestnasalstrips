# How Nasal Strips Work (and When They Don't)

## What are nasal strips?

Nasal strips are spring-loaded adhesive bands worn across the bridge of the nose. As the band tries to straighten, it gently lifts the sides of the nose outward, widening the nasal valve, the narrowest part of the airway, so air moves more easily. They are drug-free, over-the-counter, and typically single-use.

## Do nasal strips work for snoring?

They work when snoring starts in the nose, such as from congestion, allergies, or a narrow nasal valve, by widening the passage and reducing the airflow turbulence that vibrates tissue. They do not help snoring from the soft palate, tongue, or obstructive sleep apnea. If you gasp, choke, or stop breathing during sleep, see a doctor instead of using a strip.

## Nasal strips vs nasal dilators

Internal dilators expand the nostril from the inside and tend to deliver stronger, steadier airflow, a 2019 study found internal dilators outperformed adhesive strips. Adhesive strips are more comfortable, more hygienic, and easier to travel with. Reusable external dilators like Intake aim to split the difference.

- Choose a strip for: lowest upfront cost, nothing inside the nose, single-use hygiene, easy travel.
- Choose a dilator for: strongest airflow, staying put during movement/sweat, lower cost per night, no adhesive.

## Are nasal strips safe nightly?

For most people, yes. The main downside of nightly adhesive use is skin irritation or mild redness where the adhesive pulls. Sensitive skin does better with gentle-adhesive strips or a reusable dilator that uses no adhesive. Stop if you develop a rash.
